# Research Evidence Checklist

## Frame before searching

Write the research goal in terms of an outcome, population or system, mechanism, constraint, and time or operating boundary. A broad objective is useful, but an evidence review needs an answerable question. Record which statements came from the requester and which are assumptions introduced to make the question testable.

Separate four labels throughout the packet:

- **Observed fact**: directly supported by a supplied record or a cited source.
- **Inference**: a reasoned interpretation of one or more observed facts.
- **Hypothesis**: a falsifiable proposed explanation or intervention.
- **Unknown**: a decision-relevant fact that is absent, stale, blocked, or conflicting.

## Evaluate evidence

For every important source, record its origin, publication or retrieval date, method when known, population or system context, and limitation. Prefer primary research, official technical documentation, datasets, and direct measurements for claims that depend on them. A review can orient a search but does not substitute for its underlying evidence.

Check relevance, methodology, comparability, recency, independence, and conflict. Do not turn an abstract, a search snippet, an unreplicated finding, or an inaccessible page into proof. If evidence is blocked or unavailable, the evidence ledger should say so explicitly.

## Review questions

Before ranking an idea, ask: What would change the conclusion? What alternative mechanism explains the same observation? What control, baseline, or sensitivity check is missing? Does the proposed test pose an ethics, safety, privacy, or approval dependency? The review should make the uncertainty visible instead of hiding it in a confidence score.
